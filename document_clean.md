—# Large-Scale Animal Impound Handbook

*A practical guide for agencies, law enforcement, and partners responding to large-scale animal impound, cruelty, hoarding, and disaster cases in Colorado.*

---

## Introduction

The Large-Scale Animal Impound Handbook was developed to guide agencies and organizations through the challenges of responding to large-scale animal events, such as impounds, cruelty seizures, hoarding cases, or disasters. These situations demand rapid coordination, cross-agency collaboration, and thoughtful execution under pressure. This handbook offers a clear, practical framework that promotes effective, humane, and scalable responses, grounded in shared principles, legal awareness, and operational readiness.

This resource is designed to support these goals:

- Consistent practices across agencies, municipalities, and departments
- Common language, protocols, checklists, and documentation templates
- Seamless collaboration between enforcement, legal teams, sheltering partners, and communication professionals
- Evidence documentation, chain of custody, coordination with prosecutors, and public messaging guidance

Successful outcomes hinge on seamless collaboration. The handbook includes guidance from the initial scene response to long-term animal care and case resolution.

## Start Here: Animal Case Initiation Decision-Making Framework

**Before any action is taken, the investigating agency should establish that there is legal justification to intervene.**

Key factors experts consider for probable cause:

- Observable evidence shows violations of animal welfare laws
- Animals show signs of cruelty, neglect, or suffering
- Documented evidence (photos, video, veterinary assessments) support findings

Use the Three Critical Questions flowchart (detailed in Section 2) as your primary decision tool.

**The Three Critical Questions:**

1. Is there probable cause—
2. Do the animals need to be removed—
3. Is ongoing care sufficiently resourced—

If all three cannot be answered affirmatively with confidence, follow the six Actions outlined in the handbook before proceeding with removal.

# Section 1: Preparedness — Before a Case Begins

## 1.1 Building a Regional Partner Network

Building a regional partner network is essential for successful large-scale responses. No single agency has all the resources needed. Pre-established relationships with law enforcement, shelters, veterinarians, legal experts, and funders dramatically improve outcomes.

## Building Long-Term Capacity

### Build Financial Reserves
Agencies should maintain dedicated reserves or access to emergency funds for large-scale events. Cost of care can quickly reach tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars.

### Increase Physical Capacity
Identify temporary housing options, transport resources, and supply stockpiles in advance.

## 1.2 Incident Command System: Resources For Getting Started

Adopt or align with ICS principles for large incidents. Clear command structure, defined roles, and scalable organization prevent chaos.

## 1.3 Assessing Agency Capacity and Resources

Agencies responding to large-scale animal cases frequently face a core operational reality: the resources required to impound and care for large numbers of animals — space, staff, and funding — often exceed what is immediately available.

Yet when animals are suffering or in imminent danger, neither moral obligation nor legal authority permits inaction.

Use the Resource Assessment Tool in the Appendix to evaluate readiness.

## When Resources are Inadequate: Options to Consider

- Build capacity before acting
- Document the decision not to act
- Partner with other agencies
- Seek temporary restraining orders or other legal tools

## 1.4 Financial Readiness: Budgeting and Cost Projection

Understanding costs before a case begins is critical.

**Key Cost Categories:**
- Initial intake costs (one-time)
- Daily boarding costs (ongoing)
- Medical care costs (as needed)
- Other allowable costs

Utilize financial experts early. See the Cost of Care section for detailed tools.

# Section 2: Case Initiation — Deciding the Course of Action

## 2.1 Before a Removal Decision Is Made

The impound is not inevitable. Consider alternatives to removal when appropriate, including compliance agreements, education, or targeted interventions.

**When Removal is Unlikely to Be Warranted**
- Minor issues with willing owners
- Situations where in-place remedies are sufficient

## 2.2 The Three Critical Questions

Before any action can be taken, the investigating agency should establish legal justification.

**The Three Critical Questions:**

1. **Is there probable cause—**
2. **Do the animals need to be removed—**
3. **Is ongoing care sufficiently resourced—**

A detailed decision flowchart is provided in the handbook.

**If All Three Critical Questions Cannot Be Answered:**

Follow these six Actions:

- **Action 1:** Stop and reassess
- **Action 2:** Document everything thoroughly
- **Action 3:** Contact expert partners
- **Action 4:** Assess resources realistically
- **Action 5:** Develop a coordinated plan
- **Action 6:** If animals are in immediate danger, take necessary protective steps while documenting

## 2.3 Understanding Cruelty vs. Neglect

Distinguish between cruelty (intentional harm) and neglect (failure to provide care). Different legal thresholds and responses may apply.

**When Removal Decisions Are Not Straightforward**

Partial removal cases are common: only some animals meet the threshold for removal.

## 2.4 What Constitutes a Valid Complaint & Establishing Probable Cause

- Document observable conditions
- Obtain veterinary assessments when possible
- Collect photos, video, and witness statements
- Follow proper notice procedures

## 2.5 Assessing Urgency, Danger, and Removal Timelines

Use the Removal Timeline Decision Tree.

**Quick Danger Assessment** factors include:

- Immediate life-threatening conditions
- Severe suffering
- Number of animals and available resources

Special considerations for exigent circumstances.

## 2.6 Coordinating with Expert Partners

Obtain legal, operational, medical, and financial expertise **before** acting in complex cases.

What expert partners provide is detailed in the handbook.
